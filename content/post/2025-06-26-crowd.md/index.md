## Blair
I attended the magnet program at Montgomery Blair High School in Montgomery County, MD, known for its nationally renowned, highly selective program focused on STEM. With a hundred students across each grade, it's full of all kinds of brilliant students: math nerds, physics nerds, debate nerds, robotics nerds, programming nerds, writing nerds, and more. With just a four-hundred student body, Blair is hyper-competitive on the national academic stage, harboring students that regularly place in the top ranks in national or international math competitions, physics competitions, and debate tournaments. In any academic or competitive domain, you'd find at least a handful of top-performers somewhere in the Blair magnet program. 

The program offers a unique academic curriculum, full of rigorous coursework in advanced mathematics, computer science, biology, and physics with a strong emphasis on independent research that is seldomly found in any high school environment. We had access to unique courses you'd typically only find in college like quantum physics, entomology, and complex analysis. Most of us also participated in after school clubs like math team, physics team, Science Bowl, or debate, where students routinely qualified for national tournaments and brought home titles from prestigious competitions.

You might think that with a program like that, you'd only expect to see such results. Blair students are provided with resources and courses most kids dream of having, and one of the common conversations among the public regarding magnet schools was the question of how to expand such resources to a broader population. More specifically, there's been a movement aimed at expanding access to magnet programs to a more diverse population. 

Looking at Blair's demographics, it's not hard to see that most of the student body is white and Asian (mainly Chinese) with a stark absence of other minority groups. 

To consider Blair for its academic curriculum is to unequivocally miss the point. It relies on the assumption that the program itself is what makes the students great, which is completely wrong. The reason is reverse-causal--the students make the program great, not the other way around. The program is simply a way to bring intelligent, ambitious, and talented students together so they feed off one another and make each other stronger. According to Wikipedia,
> Magnet schools are public schools with specialized courses or curricula.
These schools are meant to attract ambitious students who care and excel in their programs. The idea is to give these students additional resources to explore their interests and gain a more rigorous understanding of certain materials. 

There has been a recent disturbance in the admission policies of magnet schools. Thomas Jefferson High School for Science and Technology, historically known for its competitive admission process unbelievably high-performing student body, has moved their admission system to a lottery-based system. My magnet middle school and high school, Takoma Park Middle School and Montgomery Blair High School, were the beacons of high STEM performance in my county. Now, they've reduced their admission policies to no-test, essay- and loterry-based systems.

Previously, these programs focused on admitting both talented and ambitious students. Now, the emphasis is solely on admitting ambitious students. That's not that big of a difference, is it? If the goal is to help students explore their passion in STEM or literature or whatnot, why does talent matter? 

# Talent Matters
The smartest people I have ever met in my life are from my middle and high school, and it's not even close. I've worked three internships, attended a semi-prestigious university with 30,000+ students, participated in my fair share of clubs and events, and no one has even stepped in the realm of the people I met in my *high school.* A question begs: was Blair's program so good that it churned out all of these execellent students, or did these excellent students simply happen to attend Blair? 

I rode the bus with an International Math Olympiad Gold Medalist. A plurality of my friends are interning in the world's most prestigious market makers and trading firms.
